# The Product

## Track chain links for heavy earthmoving equipment

The forging line produces **track chain links** — the individual steel pieces that, once assembled into a continuous chain, form the undercarriage track system of heavy earthmoving machinery such as excavators, bulldozers, and crawler loaders.

## From raw steel to moving earth

### The forged link

Each piece starts as a heated steel billet that enters the forging line. Through a sequence of press strikes and drilling on the main press, the billet is shaped into a single track link with its characteristic profile: pin holes on each end for connecting to adjacent links, and a flat contact surface that interfaces with the track shoe.

### The link pair

Two links are assembled together — an inner link and an outer link — joined by pins and bushings. This forms a single articulating unit of the track chain.

<img src="assets/track_chain_link_pair.png" alt="A pair of finished track chain links, ready for chain assembly" width="800">

*A finished link pair showing the interlocking geometry. The pin bores allow articulation between adjacent pairs, enabling the chain to wrap around the drive sprocket and idler wheels.*

### The assembled track chain

Dozens of link pairs are connected in sequence to form a complete track chain loop. The chain wraps around the drive sprocket (powered by the hydraulic motor), the front idler, and the track rollers underneath the machine frame.

<img src="assets/track_chain_assembled.png" alt="A complete track chain loop assembled from individual forged links" width="800">

*A complete track chain ready for installation. Each link in this chain was forged, heat-treated, and finished on the production line monitored by this project's data.*

### The final application

The track chain is a critical component of the undercarriage of heavy earthmoving equipment. It must withstand extreme loads, abrasive terrain, and continuous operation in harsh environments. The quality and consistency of the forging process directly impacts the chain's durability and the machine's operational reliability.

<img src="assets/excavator_undercarriage.png" alt="An excavator at work, showing the track chain system on the undercarriage" width="800">

*A heavy excavator in operation. The track chains are visible on both sides of the undercarriage, carrying the full weight of the machine and providing traction on rough terrain.*

## The manufacturing process

The forging line transforms heated steel billets into finished track links through four main zones:

1. **Furnace** — billets are heated to forging temperature in an induction furnace
2. **Main press** — the heated billet is stamped in four consecutive operations (1st strike -upsetting, 2nd strike - first operation, 3rd strike - second operation, and drill) that form the link geometry
3. **Auxiliary press** — deburring and coining to clean edges and refine dimensions
4. **Quench bath** — thermal treatment to achieve the required hardness and metallurgical properties

The entire journey from furnace exit to quench bath takes approximately **56–59 seconds** per piece, with a new piece exiting the line every **11–16 seconds**.

## Why process data matters

Every link in the chain must meet tight tolerances and metallurgical specifications. If the forging process deviates — for example, if a piece spends too long between the furnace and the press, or if a press operation is delayed — the thermal conditions change and the piece quality may be compromised.

This is why monitoring the **per-piece travel time** through each stage of the forging line is operationally critical: it allows detecting process deviations in real time, identifying which segment of the line is causing delays, and correlating those delays with potential root causes in the equipment.
